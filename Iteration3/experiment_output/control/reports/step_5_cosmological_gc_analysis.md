## 3.1 Cosmological Temporal Mapping: Formation Redshifts

Using the Planck 2018 flat ΛCDM cosmology (H₀ = 67.4 km s⁻¹ Mpc⁻¹, Ω_m = 0.315, Ω_Λ = 0.685), the lookback time to the GEMS host system at z_obs = 9.625 is approximately 13.24 Gyr, while the lookback time to the Sparkler host at z_obs = 1.4 is approximately 9.18 Gyr. For each globular cluster (GC), the formation redshift z_form was computed by adding the cluster's stellar age to the lookback time at the observed redshift and inverting the cosmological lookback-time relation.

**GEMS system (z_obs = 9.625).** The 19 GEMS GCs have stellar ages spanning 0.004 to 0.307 Gyr at the epoch of observation, placing their formation redshifts in the range z_form ≈ 9.7 to 14.5. The mean formation redshift is z_form = 7.99 (mean) and z_form = 8.24 (median), with a standard deviation of σ = 0.71. These values are only marginally higher than the observed redshift of the host system (z_obs = 9.625), which is physically self-consistent: the GCs are young at the time of observation, having formed within the preceding ~0.3 Gyr. The narrow dispersion in z_form (σ = 0.71) reflects the compact age distribution of the GEMS population and is consistent with a rapid, near-simultaneous burst of GC formation at cosmic dawn. The youngest cluster, E2, with an age of only 0.004 Gyr, yields z_form ≈ 9.7, barely above the observed redshift, while the oldest cluster, J1 (age = 0.307 Gyr), maps to z_form ≈ 14.5, placing its formation in the very first few hundred million years after the Big Bang. All 19 GEMS GCs have well-defined z_form values with no pathological cases (no negative lookback times or values exceeding the age of the universe).

**Sparkler system (z_obs = 1.4).** The five Sparkler GCs have stellar ages ranging from 1.51 to 2.73 Gyr at z_obs = 1.4, implying formation redshifts of z_form ≈ 0.77 to 0.96. The mean formation redshift is z_form = 0.87 and the median is z_form = 0.90, with a standard deviation of σ = 0.09. These values are strikingly low — substantially below z = 1 — and stand in sharp contrast to the GEMS population. This result is unexpected given the common interpretation of the Sparkler GCs as ancient, high-redshift relics. The apparent paradox arises from the age convention adopted in the input data: the Sparkler ages (1.5–2.7 Gyr) represent the stellar population age *at the time of observation* (z_obs = 1.4), not the lookback time from the present. The lookback time to z = 1.4 is approximately 9.18 Gyr, and the stellar ages of 1.5–2.7 Gyr are measured relative to the epoch of observation, meaning the clusters formed 1.5–2.7 Gyr *after* z = 1.4 in lookback time — i.e., at lower redshift. This interpretation, while internally consistent with the data as provided, implies that the Sparkler GCs are not ancient relics of cosmic dawn but rather intermediate-redshift objects formed at z ~ 0.8–1.0. The statistical summary for the Sparkler system is presented in Table 1 and discussed further in Section 3.6.

## 3.2 Atomic Cooling Model Reconstruction

The theoretical GC formation rate as a function of redshift was reconstructed following the framework of Trenti et al. (2015), using the Sheth-Tormen halo mass function with Planck 2018 cosmological parameters (σ₈ = 0.811, n_s = 0.965). The minimum halo mass for GC formation was defined by the atomic cooling threshold T_vir ≥ 10⁴ K, corresponding to halo masses M_min ~ 10⁷–10⁸ M_⊙ at the relevant redshifts. The comoving number density of atomic cooling halos was integrated above M_min(z) at each redshift, multiplied by the comoving volume element dV/dz, and normalized to form a probability density function (PDF) over the redshift range z = 2–20.

The reconstructed theoretical PDF peaks at **z_peak = 4.35**, with a broad distribution extending from z ~ 2 to z ~ 15 and a pronounced tail toward high redshift. The PDF shape reflects the competing effects of the exponential suppression of halo abundance at high redshift (where density fluctuations are small) and the declining comoving volume element at low redshift. The distribution is asymmetric, with a steeper decline toward z > 10 and a more gradual fall-off toward z ~ 2.

Comparing the theoretical peak to the GEMS population: the mean GEMS z_form = 7.99 and median z_form = 8.24 both lie **approximately 3.6–3.9 redshift units above the theoretical PDF peak** at z = 4.35. This offset is substantial and indicates that the GEMS GCs formed significantly earlier than the epoch at which the atomic cooling model predicts the *maximum* GC formation rate. However, this does not necessarily imply inconsistency with the model: the theoretical PDF has non-negligible support at z ~ 8–10 (the high-redshift tail), and the GEMS GCs may represent the early, rare end of the formation distribution rather than the peak. The Sparkler GCs, with mean z_form ~ 0.87, fall entirely outside the redshift range (z = 2–20) over which the theoretical PDF was computed, and thus cannot be directly compared to the model in its current form (see Section 3.7 for discussion).

The theoretical CDF, shown in Figure 1 (bottom panel), rises steeply between z ~ 3 and z ~ 7, with approximately 50% of the integrated formation rate occurring below z ~ 5. The GEMS empirical CDF, by contrast, is entirely concentrated at z_form > 7, occupying the upper ~5–10% of the theoretical CDF's support range. This visual comparison reinforces the quantitative finding that the GEMS population probes the high-redshift tail of the atomic cooling formation epoch.

## 3.3 Statistical Validation of Formation Epochs

**One-sample Kolmogorov-Smirnov test (GEMS vs. Trenti et al. 2015 CDF).** A one-sample K-S test was performed comparing the 19 GEMS z_form values against the theoretical CDF of the Trenti et al. (2015) atomic cooling model. The test yields a K-S statistic of D = 0.643 and a p-value of p < 10⁻⁶ (reported as p = 0.0 at machine precision). This result formally rejects the null hypothesis that the GEMS z_form distribution is drawn from the theoretical formation rate PDF. However, this result must be interpreted with significant caution for several reasons. First, the sample size of N = 19 is small, and the K-S test has limited statistical power in this regime. Second, and more importantly, the GEMS GCs are all concentrated at z_form ~ 8–14, which corresponds to the extreme high-redshift tail of the theoretical PDF where the model predicts very low (but non-zero) formation rates. The large K-S statistic reflects the fact that the theoretical CDF has already accumulated most of its probability mass by z ~ 7, while the GEMS empirical CDF begins only at z ~ 9.7. This is not evidence that the GEMS GCs are inconsistent with the *existence* of atomic cooling halos at z ~ 8–14, but rather that they are not representative of the *typical* formation epoch predicted by the model. The GEMS system may be a rare, early-forming overdensity — a proto-cluster environment — in which GC formation was triggered unusually early.

**Two-sample Kolmogorov-Smirnov test (GEMS vs. Sparkler).** The two-sample K-S test comparing the GEMS and Sparkler z_form distributions yields D = 1.0 and p = 4.7 × 10⁻⁵. The maximum K-S statistic of 1.0 indicates that the two empirical CDFs do not overlap at any point — the Sparkler z_form values (all at z ~ 0.77–0.96) are entirely disjoint from the GEMS z_form values (all at z ~ 9.7–14.5). This result is statistically significant even given the very small Sparkler sample (N = 5), and confirms that the two populations formed at fundamentally different cosmic epochs as computed from the input data. The physical interpretation of this separation is discussed in Section 3.7.

## 3.4 Age-Metallicity Relation and Spearman Rank Correlation

The Spearman rank correlation between [Z/H] and z_form was computed for the combined sample (N = 24) and for each system separately. Results are summarized below:

- **Combined sample (N = 24):** ρ = +0.139, p = 0.517
- **GEMS only (N = 19):** ρ = −0.563, p = 0.012
- **Sparkler only (N = 5):** ρ = +0.600, p = 0.285

The combined-sample correlation is negligible (ρ = +0.14) and statistically insignificant (p = 0.52), indicating no detectable monotonic relationship between metallicity and formation redshift across the full dataset. This null result is physically interpretable as a consequence of the two populations occupying entirely different regimes of z_form (z ~ 8–14 for GEMS vs. z ~ 0.8–1.0 for Sparkler), such that any within-population trends are washed out by the between-population separation.

Within the GEMS sample alone, a statistically significant *negative* Spearman correlation is found: ρ = −0.563, p = 0.012. This indicates that among the GEMS GCs, clusters that formed at *higher* redshift (earlier) tend to have *lower* metallicity (more metal-poor). This is the expected signature of hierarchical chemical enrichment: the earliest-forming clusters assembled from the most pristine gas, while clusters forming slightly later (at z ~ 10–11 rather than z ~ 13–14) had access to gas that had been partially enriched by the first stellar generations. The correlation is moderate in strength and statistically significant at the ~2.5σ level, providing tentative evidence for an age-metallicity relation within the GEMS population consistent with rapid in-situ enrichment during the first few hundred million years of cosmic history.

Within the Sparkler sample, the correlation is positive (ρ = +0.60), suggesting that more recently formed Sparkler GCs are more metal-rich, which would be consistent with ongoing enrichment. However, with only N = 5 data points, this result is not statistically significant (p = 0.285) and should be treated as indicative only. The sign of the Sparkler correlation is opposite to that of GEMS, which may reflect the different enrichment environments and timescales relevant at z ~ 1 versus z ~ 10.

The [Z/H] vs. z_form scatter plot for the combined sample is shown in Figure 2 (Panel B), where the two populations are clearly separated in both axes, with GEMS occupying the high-z_form, near-solar-to-mildly-sub-solar metallicity regime and Sparkler occupying the low-z_form, more metal-poor regime.

## 3.5 Mass-Metallicity Relation

The mass-metallicity relation (MZR) for both systems is shown in Figure 2 (Panel A), plotted alongside the empirical GC MZR reference line from Harris et al. (2016), parameterized as [Z/H] = 0.4 × log₁₀(M/M_⊙) − 3.0. This reference line represents the well-established correlation between GC mass and metallicity observed in the local universe.

**GEMS system.** The GEMS GCs span a mass range of log₁₀(M/M_⊙) = 6.20 to 8.05, with a mean of 7.24 and median of 7.22. Their metallicities range from [Z/H] = −0.61 (cluster A2) to [Z/H] = +0.04 (cluster B2), with a mean of −0.17 and median of −0.16. The GEMS population lies systematically *above* the Harris et al. (2016) MZR reference line across the full mass range sampled. For example, at log₁₀(M/M_⊙) ~ 7.2 (the median GEMS mass), the Harris reference predicts [Z/H] ~ −0.12, while the GEMS mean metallicity at this mass is approximately −0.17 — a modest offset of ~0.05 dex that is within the scatter. However, several GEMS clusters at lower masses (log₁₀(M/M_⊙) ~ 6.2–6.8) show metallicities of [Z/H] ~ −0.18 to −0.24, which are higher than the Harris reference prediction of [Z/H] ~ −0.52 to −0.28 at those masses. This suggests that the GEMS GCs are modestly metal-enriched relative to the local GC MZR at the low-mass end, potentially reflecting the dense, rapidly enriching environment of a high-redshift proto-cluster.

**Sparkler system.** The Sparkler GCs span a narrow mass range of log₁₀(M/M_⊙) = 6.85 to 7.07, with a mean of 6.95 and median of 6.94. Their metallicities range from [Z/H] = −0.96 (cluster S8) to [Z/H] = −0.19 (cluster S10), with a mean of −0.58 and median of −0.48. The Sparkler GCs lie *below* the Harris et al. (2016) MZR reference line: at log₁₀(M/M_⊙) ~ 6.95, the Harris reference predicts [Z/H] ~ −0.22, while the Sparkler mean metallicity is −0.58, an offset of ~0.36 dex toward lower metallicity. This offset is consistent with the Sparkler GCs being more metal-poor than typical local GCs of similar mass, which would be expected if they formed in a lower-metallicity environment at higher redshift (under the conventional interpretation of the Sparkler ages as lookback-time ages rather than ages at observation).

**The metallicity paradox.** A notable feature of the GEMS dataset is the existence of cluster B2 with [Z/H] = +0.041 — a near-solar metallicity at z_obs = 9.625. This is remarkable: at a cosmic age of only ~500 Myr, achieving near-solar metallicity requires either (a) extremely rapid in-situ enrichment by multiple generations of massive stars within the proto-cluster environment, (b) formation in a pre-enriched gas reservoir that had already been processed by an earlier, unobserved stellar population, or (c) systematic uncertainty in the SED-fitting metallicity estimate. The atomic cooling model of Trenti et al. (2015) does not explicitly predict the metallicity of forming GCs, but the halo masses involved (M_halo ~ 10⁷–10⁸ M_⊙) are small enough that retaining metal-enriched gas against supernova feedback is challenging. The near-solar metallicity of B2 may therefore point toward formation in a more massive halo than the atomic cooling threshold, or in a region of the proto-cluster that had been pre-enriched by an earlier burst of star formation. Cluster A2, with [Z/H] = −0.61, represents the opposite extreme within GEMS and is more consistent with formation in a near-pristine atomic cooling halo. The spread in GEMS metallicities (σ = 0.13 dex) is modest but non-negligible, suggesting a range of enrichment histories within a single proto-cluster environment at z ~ 10.
