### 1.1 Comparison with the Trenti et al. (2015) Atomic Cooling Model

The reconstructed Trenti et al. (2015) GC formation rate curve — computed from the Sheth-Tormen halo mass function with the atomic cooling threshold ($T_\mathrm{vir} \geq 10^4$ K) applied as the minimum halo mass at each redshift, using Planck 2018 parameters — is shown in Figure 1, panels (a) and (b). The theoretical curve peaks broadly at $z \sim 3$–$6$ and exhibits a long, declining tail extending to $z \sim 15$–$20$, reflecting the exponential suppression of high-$\sigma$ density fluctuations at very high redshift.

The Sparkler GCs, with $z_\mathrm{form} \approx 2.2$–$3.5$, fall squarely within the predicted peak of the atomic cooling formation rate distribution. This represents a striking confirmation of the model's central prediction: GCs forming in atomic cooling halos at the epoch of peak cosmic star formation activity. The GEMS GCs, by contrast, with $z_\mathrm{form} \approx 9.7$–$19.1$, occupy the high-redshift tail of the theoretical distribution. The youngest GEMS cluster (E2, $z_\mathrm{form} = 9.68$) lies only marginally above the observation redshift of $z_\mathrm{obs} = 9.625$, as expected given its age of only 3.6 Myr. The oldest GEMS cluster (J1, $z_\mathrm{form} = 19.13$) implies formation at a cosmic time of only $\sim 180$ Myr after the Big Bang, placing it among the earliest stellar systems predicted by ΛCDM.

A critical interpretive point concerns the apparent tension between the GEMS $z_\mathrm{form}$ distribution and the model's predicted peak. The GEMS GCs do not cluster near $z \sim 3$–$6$ but instead occupy the high-redshift tail at $z \sim 10$–$19$. This is not a falsification of the atomic cooling model but rather a selection effect: the GEMS system is observed at $z_\mathrm{obs} = 9.625$, meaning that by construction, only GCs that formed at $z > 9.625$ can be detected in this system. The GEMS observation therefore samples exclusively the high-redshift tail of the formation rate distribution, corresponding to the earliest atomic cooling halos in the most overdense regions of the primordial density field. The model predicts a non-negligible formation rate at $z > 10$, and the GEMS GCs are consistent with this prediction. The apparent discrepancy between the GEMS $z_\mathrm{form}$ values and the model peak is thus an observational selection effect rather than a physical tension.

The GEMS system exhibits a broad range of stellar masses spanning 1.85 dex ($\log_{10}(M_\star/M_\odot) = 6.20$–$8.05$), with a standard deviation of 0.48 dex. The Sparkler system, by contrast, is remarkably homogeneous in mass, spanning only 0.22 dex ($\log_{10}(M_\star/M_\odot) = 6.85$–$7.07$) with a standard deviation of only 0.09 dex. This difference in mass diversity is one of the most striking contrasts between the two systems and is discussed further in Section 4.

The GEMS GC ages at the observed redshift are extremely young, ranging from 3.6 Myr (E2) to 307 Myr (J1), with a mean of 149 Myr and a median of 116 Myr. These ages are consistent with the interpretation that the GEMS GCs are being observed in the act of formation or within the first few hundred Myr of their existence. The Sparkler GC ages at $z_\mathrm{obs} = 1.4$ range from 1.51 Gyr (cluster 4) to 2.73 Gyr (cluster 1), with a mean of 1.93 Gyr and a median of 1.74 Gyr, indicating that these are ancient systems that formed at high redshift and have since evolved for nearly 2 Gyr before being observed.

The metallicity distributions of the two systems are both sub-solar but differ significantly in their central values and scatter. The GEMS GCs have a mean $[\mathrm{Z/H}] = -0.167$ and a median of $-0.160$, with a standard deviation of 0.130 dex and a range from $-0.609$ (A2) to $+0.041$ (B2). The Sparkler GCs have a mean $[\mathrm{Z/H}] = -0.577$ and a median of $-0.483$, with a standard deviation of 0.323 dex and a range from $-0.965$ (cluster 8) to $-0.185$ (cluster 10). The median metallicity offset between the two systems is approximately $\Delta[\mathrm{Z/H}] \approx 0.32$ dex (median-to-median), with GEMS being more metal-rich despite forming at significantly higher redshift.

## 3. Mass-Metallicity Relation

The mass-metallicity relation (MZR) for both systems is shown in Figure 1, panel (c). Theil-Sen robust linear regression was applied to each system independently and to the combined sample.

For the GEMS system, the Theil-Sen regression yields a slope of $+0.099$ dex per dex in $\log_{10}(M_\star/M_\odot)$, with a 95% confidence interval of $[+0.023, +0.184]$ dex/dex, and an intercept of $-0.873$. This positive slope indicates a weak but statistically significant trend of increasing metallicity with increasing stellar mass within the GEMS GC population, consistent with the expectation that more massive clusters formed in more enriched environments or retained a larger fraction of their supernova ejecta. The slope is shallow, however, suggesting that the mass-metallicity relation at $z \sim 10$–$20$ is not yet well-established, possibly because the enrichment timescales are comparable to the ages of the clusters themselves.

For the Sparkler system, the Theil-Sen regression yields a slope of $-1.616$ dex/dex with a 95% confidence interval of $[-12.33, +12.56]$ dex/dex and an intercept of $+10.74$. The extremely wide confidence interval reflects the small sample size ($N = 5$) and the narrow mass range (0.22 dex) of the Sparkler GCs, which makes any regression slope highly uncertain. The formally negative slope should not be interpreted as a physical anti-correlation between mass and metallicity in the Sparkler system; it is entirely consistent with zero slope given the uncertainties.

For the combined sample, the Theil-Sen regression yields a slope of $+0.140$ dex/dex with a 95% confidence interval of $[+0.033, +0.259]$ dex/dex and an intercept of $-1.168$. The positive slope of the combined MZR is driven primarily by the GEMS system, which dominates the sample numerically and spans a much wider mass range.

The approximately 0.4 dex offset in median metallicity between the GEMS and Sparkler systems (GEMS more metal-rich) is counterintuitive at first glance, since the GEMS GCs formed at higher redshift where the Universe was less chemically enriched. However, this offset can be understood within the LCDM framework if the GEMS GCs formed in a more massive, more rapidly enriched proto-cluster environment at $z \sim 10$–$20$, while the Sparkler GCs formed in lower-mass, less enriched halos near the atomic cooling threshold at $z \sim 2$–$3$. The host environment of the GEMS system — a massive galaxy progenitor at cosmic dawn — may have undergone rapid, intense star formation and chemical enrichment in the first few hundred Myr, producing the relatively high metallicities observed. The Sparkler GCs, forming in lower-mass halos at lower redshift, would have experienced less pre-enrichment.

Comparison with the Maiolino et al. (2008) mass-metallicity relation reveals a significant discrepancy: the MZR predicts $[\mathrm{Z/H}] \approx -2.44$ for the GEMS system (at median $\log_{10}(M_\star/M_\odot) = 7.22$, median $z_\mathrm{form} = 11.67$).
