The observed tension between the GEMS and Sparkler populations arises because the atomic cooling model (Trenti et al. 2015) describes the *initial* formation of globular clusters in low-mass halos at high redshift, but fails to account for the *secondary* formation channel driven by the hierarchical assembly of massive galaxies. I hypothesize that the Sparkler GCs are not "survivors" of a high-redshift population, but rather represent a distinct "second generation" of clusters formed in situ within the high-pressure, metal-enriched environments of massive proto-galactic disks at $z \approx 2-6$. To test this, I propose a "Specific Frequency vs. Host Mass" analysis: by calculating the ratio of the observed GC stellar mass to the estimated host galaxy mass (using the observed mass-metallicity relation as a proxy for the host's chemical maturity), we can determine if the Sparkler clusters follow a different scaling law compared to the GEMS clusters. If the Sparkler clusters exhibit a significantly higher mass-efficiency (GC mass per unit host mass) than the GEMS clusters, it would confirm that they formed via a different physical mechanism—likely gas-rich disk fragmentation—rather than the atomic cooling halo channel, thereby resolving the "bifurcation" identified in previous iterations.
