The observed tension between the Sparkler GCs and the atomic cooling model arises because the atomic cooling threshold ($T_{vir} \geq 10^4$ K) only describes the initial formation epoch, while the Sparkler population represents a "surviving" subset biased by hierarchical assembly and dynamical mass loss. I hypothesize that the metallicity offset and the $z_{form}$ discrepancy can be reconciled by applying a mass-dependent survival probability function to the Trenti et al. (2015) model. Specifically, by weighting the theoretical formation rate by a survival factor $S(M, z) \propto M^{\alpha}(1+z)^{\beta}$, we can test if the Sparkler GCs are the high-mass, high-metallicity tail of the early-forming population that survived to $z=1.4$, whereas the GEMS population represents the raw, unfiltered formation rate at $z \approx 10$. This hypothesis will be tested by comparing the observed mass-metallicity-age distribution against a survival-weighted theoretical model to see if the "tension" is actually a signature of preferential cluster disruption in the early universe.
